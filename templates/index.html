Actinic keratoses

Pre-malignant condition

An actinic keratosis (AK), also known as a solar keratosis, is a crusty, scaly growth caused by damage from exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

The most common type of precancerous skin lesion, in the beginning, AKs are frequently so small that they are recognized by touch rather than sight. They feel as if you were running a finger over sandpaper. Patients may have many times more invisible (subclinical) lesions than those appearing on the surface.

Causes: Sun exposure, indoor tanning, extensive exposure to X-rays

Who is most at risk: People with fair skin, freckles, blonde or red hair and blue, green or gray eyes

How to spot: Typically occur on the face, lips, ears, bald scalp, shoulders, neck and back of the hands and forearms

Basal cell carcinoma

A malignant form of skin lesion

BCCs are abnormal, uncontrolled growths or lesions that arise in the skin’s basal cells, which line the deepest layer of the epidermis (the outermost layer of the skin).

BCCs often look like open sores, red patches, pink growths, shiny bumps, or scars. BCCs are easily treated in their early stages. The larger the tumor has grown, however, the more extensive the treatment needed.

Causes: Combination of cumulative and intense, occasional sun exposure

Who is most at risk: Anyone with a history of sun exposure

How to spot: Typically occur on the sun-exposed areas of the body

Benign keratosis

A benign form of skin lesion

A seborrheic keratosis usually appears as a brown, black or light tan growth. The growth has a waxy, scaly, slightly elevated appearance. Seborrheic keratoses don't become cancerous and aren't thought to be related to sun exposure, but they can look like skin cancer.

Seborrheic keratoses are normally painless and require no treatment.

Causes: The exact cause isn't known

Who is most at risk: People over 50

How to spot: Typically occur on the face, chest, shoulders or back

Dermatofibroma

A benign form of skin lesion

Dermatofibromas are harmless growths within the skin that usually have a small diameter. They can vary in color, and the color may change over the years.

Dermatofibromas are firm to the touch. They are very dense, and many people say they feel like a small stone underneath or raised above the skin. Most dermatofibromas are painless.

Causes: They may be caused by an adverse reaction to a small injury, such as a bug bite, splinter, or puncture wound

Who is most at risk: Dermatofibromas are seen in adults but are rare in children. They are more frequent in women than in men and are more common in people with compromised immune systems

How to spot: It can develop anywhere on the body but most often appear on the lower legs, upper arms or upper back

Melanoma

Skin cancer

The most important warning sign of melanoma is a new spot on the skin or a spot that is changing in size, shape, or color. Another important sign is a spot that looks different from all of the other spots on your skin.

Other signs of melanoma include – also known as the ABCDE signs: asymmetry of lesions, border changes, irregular colour changes, a growing diameter, evolving lesions

Causes: The exact cause of all melanomas isn't clear, but exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from sunlight or tanning lamps and beds increases your risk of developing melanoma

Who is most at risk: People with fair skin, freckles, blonde or red hair and blue, green or gray eyes

How to spot: Everyone is at some risk for melanoma, but increased risk depends on several factors: sun exposure, number of moles on the skin, skin type and family history (genetics)

Melanocytic nevi

A melanocytic nevus (also known as nevocytic nevus, nevus-cell nevus and commonly as a mole) is a type of melanocytic tumor that contains nevus cells.

The majority of moles appear during the first two decades of a person's life, with about one in every 100 babies being born with moles. Acquired moles are a form of benign neoplasm, while congenital moles, or congenital nevi, are considered a minor malformation or hamartoma and may be at a higher risk for melanoma.

A mole can be either subdermal (under the skin) or a pigmented growth on the skin, formed mostly of a type of cell known as a melanocyte.

Causes: Genetics, sunlight, hormones

How to spot: Can appear anywhere on the body, but it is more often found on the trunk or limbs

Vascular lesions

Vascular lesions are among the most common congenital and neonatal abnormalities.

Vascular lesions are relatively common abnormalities of the skin and underlying tissues, more commonly known as birthmarks. There are three major categories of vascular lesions: Hemangiomas, Vascular Malformations, and Pyogenic Granulomas.

How to spot: These anomalies can occur throughout the whole body, with 60%, however, being located in the head and neck region
